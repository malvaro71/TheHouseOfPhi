Vectors in Space

In this first chapter, we examine the concepts of vector, the Cartesian coordinate system and the main vector operations, which are key mathematical tools for dealing with vectors in more than one dimension.

Vectors

A vector is a directed segment, one end of which (the point A) is said to be the origin, while the other (the point B) is said to be the end of the vector. Such a vector may be denoted by v, v⃗ or AB→. A vector whose origin and end coincide is said to be a zero vector, and is usually denoted by 0. The vector BA→ is said to be the vector opposite to AB→. A vector with length equal to one is called a unit vector. Any direction may be assigned to a zero vector.

The vector origin is also named initial point or point of application.

A geometric vector, as defined above, is also called a fixed vector or bound vector. Because it has a unique initial point. In physics is used, for example, to specify the action of the force on deformable or non rigid body. In this instance, the forces and deformations within the body depend on the point of application of the force, as well as on the magnitude and line of action.

A class of equal vectors lying on the same straight line is said to be a sliding vector. An example of sliding vector is the action of a force on a rigid body. So, in this case, the point of application can be moved along the line determined by the vector direction.

If vectors are only characterized by magnitude and direction, then are called free vectors. Examples of free vectors in physics are displacement, velocity and magnetic or electric field strength. In these cases, the vector can be moved to any point in the space

Vector calculus, which is the study of operations performed on vectors, is based on free vectors, since two given free vectors are equivalent to a given sliding vector or a given bound vector, as long as we keep in mind the physical meaning of the vector under analysis. From now on, we'll talk about free vectors if not mentioned otherwise.

A vector is characterized:

  • by its magnitude (or length), which is equal to the length of the segment AB and is denoted by `abs(vecv)`,
  • and by its direction: from A to B.

Vector Addition, Graphical Method:

The sum of two vectors `veca` and `vecb`, or vector addition, produces a third vector `vec(a+b)`, the resultant vector.`veca`

To find `vec(a+b)`, we first draw the vector `veca`, and from the terminal end of `veca`, we drawn the vector `vecb`. In other words, we have the initial point of `vecb` meet the terminal end of `veca`. This position corresponds to the notion that we move along the first vector and then, from its terminal point, we move along the second vector.

A scalar is a quantity that has magnitude but no direction. Or, in other words, a quantity that can be expressed by one (real) number.

Cartesian coordinate System

To specify the magnitude and direction of a vector we need a coordinate system. There are three commonly used coordinate systems: Cartesian, cylindrical and spherical. In this section we describe the Cartesian coordinate system.

A Cartesian coordinate system in two dimensions is defined by an ordered pair of perpendicular lines (axes), a single unit of length for both axes, and an orientation for each axis. The point where the axes meet is taken as the origin for both, thus turning each axis into a number line.

For any point P, a line is drawn through P perpendicular to each axis, and the position where it meets the axis is interpreted as a number. The two numbers, in that chosen order, are the Cartesian coordinates (x, y) of P.

A Cartesian coordinate system in three dimensions consists of three perpendicular axes: the x-axis, the y-axis, and the z-axis that go through a common point (the origin 0). Because each axis is a number line representing all real numbers in `RR`, the three-dimensional system is often denoted by `RR^3`.

The cartesian coordinate system is also called a rectangular coordinate system or orthogonal coordinate system.

In Figure 1.3(a), the positive z-axis is shown above the plane containing the x- and y-axes. The system displayed follows the right-hand rule. If we take our right hand and align the fingers with the positive x-axis, then curl the fingers so they point in the direction of the positive y-axis, our thumb points in the direction of the positive z-axis. In this website, we always work with coordinate systems set up in accordance with the right-hand rule. Some systems do follow a left-hand rule, but the right-hand rule is considered the standard representation.

We describe a point in the three-dimensional rectangular coordinate system with the coordinates (x, y, z). Each coordinate describes how the point aligns with the corresponding axis.

Components of a Vector

When the initial point of a vector coincides with the origin of the coordinate system, we call the vector a standard-position vector. Because the initial point of any vector in standard position is known to be (0,0), we can describe the vector by looking at the coordinates of its terminal point. Thus, if vector `vecv` has its initial point at the origin and its terminal point at (x, y), we write the vector in component form as

 `vecv = << x,y >>`.

When a vector is written in component form like this, the scalars x and y are called the components of `vecv`. When we need to perform calculations with vectors, we usually describe them as standard-position vectors because it is easier to perform such calculations.

Relationship between vector components and its magnitude and orientation

To find vector magnitude from its components, we use the Pythagorean Theorem. And to find its direction we use the inverse tangent function.

Given a position vector `vecv = << x,y >>`, the magnitude (or module) is found by `abs(vecv) = sqrt(x^2+y^2)`. And the direction is found by `theta = tan^1 (y/x)`.

Given the magnitude and direction of a vector, its x component is found by `x = costheta` and its y component is found `y = sintheta`.

Vector addition and scalar multiplication using vector components

The sum of two vectors given in its component form, `vecv = << x_1, y_1 >> ` and w = ` << x_2, y_2 >> `, is defined as

v + w = ` << x_1 + x_2, y_1 + y_2 >> `.

The product of an scalar `lambda` and a vector v, given in its component form, v = ` << x, y >> `, is defined as

`lambda`v = ` << lambdax, lambday >> `

The unit vector

A vector divided by its magnitude is a unit vector. The vector magnitude is an scalar, and dividing by a scalar is the same as multiplying by the reciprocal of the scalar.

Graphical definitions of Sine, Cosine and Tangent Functions

Let's consider a circunference with radius of length r with centre at the origin O in a rectangular coordinate system, and a point P with coordinates (x,y) in the circunference. And let's name α the angle between the x-axis and the segment OP. Then, sin α is the ratio y/r, cos α is the ratio x/r and tan α is the ratio y/x.

Exercise 1

A boatman is rowing on the boat, wanting to always stay perpendicular to the river bank and crossing with an average speed of 36 km / h. The river water flows with a speed of 9 km / h. How fast should the boat be propelled? In what direction?

Lets represent the river in a coordinate plane where the water flows in same direction thant the x-axis, and the y-axis is perpendicular to the river bank. Then, we represent vr as water velocity and v as the velocity with which the boat is propelled. And decompose v into x and y parts.

Then,

vr = (9 Km / h, 0),

v = (vx , vy),

and the velocity of the boat wil be

v + vr = (9 + vx, vy).

So

9 + vx = 0 (to stay perpendicular to the river)

and

vy = 36 km / h,

v = (-9 Km / h , 36 Km / h)

The magnitude of the vector v is the speed at wich the boat is propelled and it is expresed as |v|.

|v| = √vx2 + vy2 = √81+1296 = √1377 = 37.11 Km/h.

The value of the angle α is the direction in wich the boat is propeled.

α = arctan ( v y v x ) = arctan ( 36 − 9 ) = − 76 O %alpha = arctan( { v_{y} } over {v_{x}} ) = arctan( {36} over {-9} ) = -76^{O}

Here is important to consider that:

  • There are many angles which its tangent is -4 (or any other value). For the tangent, the firs two angles are θ and θ + 180º.
  • The angle value given by the calculator is measured from positive side of the x axis.

Then, first angle is -76º and second angle is 104º. Both measured from the positive side of the x axis. But the angle α represented in the drawing is measured between vx and v. So, its value is 76º.
And the boat must be propeled at a speed of 37.11 Km/h and in a direction that forms 76º with the port side of the river bank.

Exercise 2

An object moves in such a way that its velocity at a certain instant is 120 m/s and forms an angle of 30º with the horizontal. Find the horizontal and vertical components of the velocity. Express the velocity vector as a function of its components.

The formulas to calculate the components of a vector v given its magnitude r and direction θ are:
vx = r·cosθ
vy = r·senθ
v = vx + vy
Where r = 120 m/s, and θ = 30º. So
vx = 120·cos30º = 120· 3 2 = 103.9 m/s
vy = 120·sen30º = 120· 1 2 = 60 m/s
