# Vectors

## 1. Introduction
Have you ever wondered how physicists describe the motion of a planet, the path of a ball, or the trajectory of a rocket? The key lies in a fundamental concept: **position**. Knowing "where" something is in the universe, at a specific moment and relative to a reference point, is the starting point.

Position is not just a piece of data; it is the foundation upon which pillars of physics such as **displacement**, **velocity**, and **acceleration** are built. Without it, it would be impossible to understand if an object has moved, in what direction, or how fast. But its importance goes beyond motion: it directly influences **potential energy**, how **forces** are applied and felt, and the description of gravitational, electric, or magnetic **fields**.

To handle this information precisely, physics uses an indispensable mathematical tool: **position vectors**. These not only tell us how far an object is from an origin but also in what direction and sense it is located. They are the key to representing locations in two or three dimensions completely and rigorously.

The use of vectors extends far beyond kinematics. In **dynamics**, forces are vector quantities that determine the acceleration of bodies according to Newton's laws. In **statics**, the analysis of structures depends on the vector sum of forces and moments being zero. In **electromagnetism**, both the electric and magnetic fields are vector fields that describe how charges interact in space. Likewise, fundamental principles such as the conservation of linear momentum (in collisions) or angular momentum (in rotations) require rigorous vector treatment for their correct formulation.

In this first chapter, we will thoroughly explore **vectors** and the **Cartesian coordinate system**, unraveling the fundamental **vector operations** such as addition, subtraction, dot product, and cross product. These tools of **vector algebra** not only greatly simplify the resolution of complex problems in physics and engineering but also allow us, for example, to calculate the precise distance between two points with astonishing mathematical elegance. Get ready to discover the language that allows us to describe and predict the physical world with unprecedented clarity.

## 2. Vectors
A **vector** is a directed segment, one of whose ends (point A) is said to be the origin, while the other (point B) is said to be the end of the vector. Such a vector can be denoted by **_v_**, $\vec v$ or $\overrightarrow{AB}$. A vector whose origin and end coincide is said to be a **zero vector**, and is generally denoted by $\vec0$. The vector $\overrightarrow{BA}$ is said to be the **opposite vector** to $\overrightarrow{AB}$. A vector with a length equal to one is called a unit vector. Any direction can be assigned to a zero vector.

The origin of the vector is also called the initial point or point of application.

![Figure 2.1: A vector is represented by a directed line segment from its initial point A to its terminal point B.](vector:svg1_1)

A geometric vector, as defined above, is also called a **fixed vector** or **bound vector**. Because it has a unique starting point. In physics, it is used, for example, to specify the action of force on a deformable or non-rigid body. In this case, the forces and deformations within the body depend on the point of application of the force, as well as the magnitude and line of action.

A class of equal vectors lying on the same straight line is said to be a **sliding vector**. An example of a sliding vector is the action of a force on a rigid body. So, in this case, the point of application can be moved along the line determined by the direction of the vector.

If vectors are only characterized by magnitude and direction, then they are called **free vectors**. Examples of free vectors in physics are displacement, velocity, and the intensity of the magnetic or electric field. In these cases, the vector can be moved to any point in space.

Vector calculus, which is the study of operations performed on vectors, is based on free vectors, since two given free vectors are equivalent to a given sliding vector or a given bound vector, as long as we take into account the physical value and meaning of the analyzed vector. From now on, **we will talk about free vectors unless otherwise mentioned**.

A vector is characterized by:

- its **magnitude (or modulus)**, which is equal to the length of the segment $\overline{AB}$ and is denoted by $\|\vec v\|$,
- and by its **direction**: from A to B.

## 3. Vector Addition, Graphical Method
The sum of two vectors $\vec a$ and $\vec b$, or vector sum, produces a third vector $\overrightarrow{a+b}$.

To find $\overrightarrow{a+b}$, we first draw the vector $\vec a$, and from the end of $\vec a$, we draw $\vec b$. In other words, we make the origin of $\vec b$ coincide with the end of $\vec a$. This position corresponds to the notion that we move along the first vector and then, from its terminal point, we move along the second vector. Vector addition has the commutative property; so, if we first draw a vector $\vec b$, and from the end of $\vec b$, we draw the vector $\vec a$, we will get $\overrightarrow{b+a}$, which is equal to $\overrightarrow{a+b}$.

![Figure 3.1: Vector Addition. Graphical Method.](vector:svg1_2)

A **scalar** is a quantity that has magnitude but no direction. Or, in other words, a quantity that can be expressed with a (real) number.

## 4. Cartesian Coordinate System
To specify the magnitude and direction of a vector we need a coordinate system. There are three commonly used coordinate systems: Cartesian, cylindrical, and spherical. In this section we describe the Cartesian coordinate system.

A **two-dimensional Cartesian coordinate system** is defined by an ordered pair of perpendicular lines (axes), a single unit of length for both axes, and an orientation for each axis. The point where the axes meet is taken as the origin of both, thus turning each axis into a number line.

For any point P, a line is drawn through P perpendicular to each axis, and the position where it meets the axis is interpreted as a number. The two numbers, in that chosen order, are the Cartesian coordinates (x, y) of P.

![Figure 4.1: Cartesian plane and Cartesian coordinates of a point.](vector:svg1_3)

A **three-dimensional Cartesian coordinate system** consists of three perpendicular axes: the x-axis, the y-axis, and the z-axis that intersect at a common point (the origin 0). Because each axis is a number line representing all real numbers in $\mathbb{R}$, the three-dimensional system is often denoted by $\mathbb{R}^3$.

The Cartesian coordinate system is also called the **rectangular coordinate system** or **orthogonal coordinate system**.

In Figure 4.2 (a), the positive z-axis is shown above the plane containing the x and y axes. The system shown follows the right-hand rule. If we take our right hand and align the fingers with the positive x-axis, then curl the fingers so they point in the direction of the positive y-axis, our thumb points in the direction of the positive z-axis. On this website, we always work with coordinate systems configured according to the right-hand rule. Some systems follow a left-hand rule, but the right-hand rule is considered the standard representation.

![Figure 4.2: (a) Three-dimensional rectangular coordinate system with the x-axis, y-axis, and z-axis. (b) The right-hand rule is used to determine the location of the coordinate axes in the standard Cartesian Plane.](/images/3DCoordSystAndRightHandRule.svg)

We describe a point P in the three-dimensional rectangular coordinate system with the coordinates $(x_1, y_1, z_1)$. Each coordinate describes how the point aligns with the corresponding axis.

![Figure 4.3: To plot point P, with coordinates (x1, y1, z1), move x1 units along the x-axis, then go y1 units in the direction of the y-axis, then move z1 units in the direction of the z-axis.](vector:svg1_5)

## 5. Vector Components
When the initial point of a vector coincides with the origin of the coordinate system, we call the vector a **standard position vector**. Because the initial point of any vector in standard position is known to be (0,0), we can describe the vector by taking only the coordinates of its terminal point. Therefore, if the vector $\vec v$ has its initial point at the origin and its terminal point at (x, y), we write the vector using its components as

$$
\vec v = (x, y) \tag{5.1}
$$

When a vector is written in this way, the scalars x and y are called **components** of $\vec v$. When we need to perform calculations with vectors, we usually describe them as standard position vectors because it is easier to perform such calculations.

<p style="text-align: right;"><a href="#vectors">Back to top of chapter</a></p>

## 6. Relationship between Components, Magnitude, and Direction
To find the magnitude of a vector from its components, we use the Pythagorean Theorem. And to find its direction we use the inverse tangent function.

### 6.1 Vector Magnitude
Given a position vector $\vec v = (x, y)$, its **magnitude (or modulus)**, **$\|\vec v\|$**, is calculated by

$$
\|\vec v\| = \sqrt{x^2+y^2} \tag{6.1}
$$

### 6.2 Vector Direction
The **direction** of the vector, or the angle it forms with the horizontal, **$\theta$**, is calculated by
 
$$
\theta = \arctan (y/x) \tag{6.2}
$$

### 6.3 Vector Components given Magnitude and Direction
Given the magnitude and direction of a vector $\vec v$, its **x-component** is calculated by

$$
x = \|\vec v\|\cos\theta \tag{6.3}
$$

and its **y-component** is calculated by

$$
y = \|\vec v\|\sin\theta \tag{6.4}
$$

## 7. Vector Addition and Multiplication by a Scalar Using Components
The **sum of two vectors** expressed by their **components**, $\vec v = (x_1, y_1)$ and $\vec w = (x_2, y_2)$, is defined as

$$
\vec v + \vec w = (x_1 + x_2, y_1 + y_2) \tag{7.1}
$$

The **product of a scalar $\lambda$ by a vector $\vec v$**, expressed by its **components**, $\vec v = (x, y)$, is defined as

$$
\lambda\vec v = (\lambda x, \lambda y) \tag{7.2}
$$

## 8. The Unit Vector
The **unit vector** $\vec u_v$ of a non-zero vector $\vec v$ is a vector that has the same direction as $\vec v$ but a magnitude of 1. The unit vector is calculated by

$$
\vec u_v = \vec v/\|\vec v\| \tag{8.1}
$$

Note that the magnitude $\|\vec v\|$ is a scalar, and dividing by a scalar is the same as multiplying by the reciprocal of the scalar $1/\|\vec v\|$.

<p style="text-align: right;"><a href="#vectors">Back to top of chapter</a></p>

## 9. Representation of Vectors using Unit Vectors $\hat\imath$, $\hat\jmath$ and $\hat k$
In a two-dimensional space, a vector can be represented by the unit vectors $\hat\imath$ and $\hat\jmath$. The unit vector $\hat\imath$ is a vector of length 1 along the x-axis, and the unit vector $\hat\jmath$ is a vector of length 1 along the y-axis.

If we have a vector given by its components $\vec v = (x, y)$, it can be represented as:

$$
\vec v =  x\hat\imath + y\hat\jmath \tag{9.1}
$$

In a three-dimensional space, a vector can be represented by unit vectors $\hat\imath$, $\hat\jmath$ and $\hat k$. The unit vector $\hat\imath$ is a vector of length 1 along the x-axis, the unit vector $\hat\jmath$ is a vector of length 1 along the y-axis, and the unit vector $\hat k$ is a vector of length 1 along the z-axis.

If we have a vector expressed by its components $\vec v = (x, y, z)$, it can be represented as:

$$
\vec v =  x\hat\imath + y\hat\jmath + z\hat k \tag{9.2}
$$

This vector sum is called a **linear combination** of the vectors $\hat\imath$, $\hat\jmath$ and $\hat k$.

<p style="text-align: right;"><a href="#vectors">Back to top of chapter</a></p>

## 10. Dot Product of Two Vectors
The **dot product** of two vectors is a number (scalar) obtained by multiplying the corresponding components of the two vectors and then adding those products.

If $\vec v = (x_1, y_1, z_1)$ and $\vec w = (x_2, y_2, z_2)$ are two vectors, then their dot product is calculated by:

$$
\vec v\cdot\vec w = x_1\cdot x_2 + y_1\cdot y_2 + z_1\cdot z_2 \tag{10.1}
$$

In terms of magnitude and direction, the dot product can also be defined as:

$$
\vec v\cdot\vec w = \|\vec v\|\cdot\|\vec w\|\cos\theta \tag{10.2}
$$

Where $\theta$ is the angle subtended from $\vec v$ to $\vec w$.

Using [10.2], we can express $\theta$ as:

$$
\theta = \arccos\frac{\vec v\cdot\vec w}{\|\vec v\|\cdot\|\vec w\|} \tag{10.3}
$$

When the dot product of two vectors is zero, the two vectors are said to be **orthogonal** or perpendicular.

<p style="text-align: right;"><a href="#vectors">Back to top of chapter</a></p>
 
## 11. Vector Projection
The **projection of $\vec w$ onto $\vec v$** or onto the line containing $\vec v$, denoted as $\text{proj}_v \vec w$, is a vector that lies on $\vec v$ and has the same direction as $\vec w$ (if the angle between $\vec v$ and $\vec w$ is less than 90 degrees) or the opposite direction to $\vec v$ (if the angle between $\vec v$ and $\vec w$ is greater than 90 degrees).

![Figure 11.1: Projection of w onto v.](vector:svg1_6)

Since the projection of $\vec w$ onto $\vec v$ is aligned with $\vec v$:

$$
\text{proj}_v \vec w = \|\text{proj}_v \vec w\|\cdot\vec u_v \tag{11.1}
$$

From the definition of [cosine](/en/geometry#31-definition-of-sine-cosine-and-tangent), it follows that the magnitude of this projection vector can be calculated as:

$$
\| \text{proj}_v \vec w \| = \|\vec w\|\cos\theta \tag{11.2}
$$

Where $\theta$ is the angle between $\vec v$ and $\vec w$.

Now, if we consider expression [10.2] for the dot product of $\vec v$ and $\vec w$, the magnitude of $\text{proj}_v \vec w$ can also be expressed as:

$$
\| \text{proj}_v \vec w \| = \frac{\vec w\cdot\vec v}{\|\vec v\|} = \vec w\cdot\vec u_v \tag{11.3}
$$

Then, using the coordinates of $\vec v$, $(x_1, y_1, z_1)$ and of $\vec w$, $(x_2, y_2, z_2)$ to calculate their dot product:

$$
\| \text{proj}_v \vec w \| = \frac{x_1\cdot x_2 + y_1\cdot y_2 + z_1\cdot z_2}{\sqrt{x_1^2+y_1^2+z_1^2}} \tag{11.4}
$$

## 12. Cross Product
The **cross product** of two vectors is a binary operation on two vectors in a three-dimensional space and is written as **$\vec v \times \vec w$**. The result of this cross product is a third vector that is perpendicular (or orthogonal) to the vectors being multiplied, or in other words, normal to the plane containing them.

The direction of the cross product is determined by the right-hand rule. If you point your index finger in the direction of $\vec v$, and your middle finger in the direction of $\vec w$, your thumb points in the direction of $(\vec v \times \vec w)$.

If $\vec v$ and $\vec w$ are two vectors, and $\theta$ is the angle between them, the magnitude of their cross product is given by:

$$
\| \vec v \times \vec w \| = \|\vec v\|\cdot\|\vec w\|\sin\theta \tag{12.1}
$$

Thus, the magnitude (or length) of the cross product can be interpreted as the area of the parallelogram spanned by the two vectors being multiplied.

![Figure 12.1: The magnitude of the cross product as the area of the parallelogram.](vector:svg1_7)

In terms of their components, if $\vec v = (x_1, y_1, z_1)$ and $\vec w = (x_2, y_2, z_2)$, the cross product $\vec v \times \vec w$ is a vector defined as:

$$
\vec v \times \vec w = (y_1 \cdot z_2 - z_1 \cdot y_2, z_1 \cdot x_2 - x_1 \cdot z_2, x_1 \cdot y_2 - y_1 \cdot x_2) \tag{12.2}
$$

Each component of this resulting vector is the determinant of a 2x2 matrix formed by eliminating the row and column of the corresponding component from a 3x3 matrix containing the unit vectors $\hat\imath$, $\hat\jmath$, and $\hat k$ in the first row, the components of $\vec v$ in the second row, and the components of $\vec w$ in the third row.

$$
\begin{vmatrix}\vec i & \vec j & \vec k \\ x_1 & y_1 & z_1 \\ x_2 & y_2 & z_2\\ \end{vmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix} y_1 & z_1 \\ y_2 & z_2 \\ \end{vmatrix} \hat\imath - \begin{vmatrix} x_1 & z_1 \\ x_2 & z_2\\ \end{vmatrix} \hat\jmath + \begin{vmatrix} x_1 & y_1 \\ x_2 & y_2 \\ \end{vmatrix}\hat k \tag{12.3}
$$

Properties of the cross product:
- Anti-commutative property: $\vec u \times \vec v = -\vec v \times \vec u$
- Distributive property: $\vec u \times (\vec v + \vec w) = \vec u \times \vec v + \vec u \times \vec w$
- Multiplication by a scalar: $c\cdot(\vec u \times \vec v) = (c\cdot\vec u) \times \vec v = \vec u \times (c\cdot\vec v)$
- Cross product of the zero vector: $\vec u \times \vec 0 = \vec 0 \times \vec u = \vec 0$
- Cross product of a vector with itself: $\vec v \times \vec v = \vec 0$

## 13. Moment of a Sliding Vector about a Point
The **moment of a sliding vector** $\vec v$ about a point P is calculated by the cross product of $\vec r$ and $\vec v$. Where $\vec r$ is the position vector of $\vec v$ with respect to P.

$$
\vec M = \vec r \times \vec v \tag{13.1}
$$

Where the position vector, $\vec r$, extends from the point of interest P to the origin of $\vec v$.

![Figure 13.1: Moment of a sliding vector v about a point P.](vector:svg1_8)

The properties of the cross product allow us to conclude that $\vec M$ is a vector perpendicular to the plane defined by $\vec r$ and $\vec v$ and that it does not change if we move the sliding vector $\vec v$ along its line of action. This is because the moment of a sliding vector is a measure of its tendency to cause rotation around a point or axis, which is determined by both the magnitude of the vector and its distance from the point or axis.

When a vector is slid along its line of action, neither the magnitude of the sliding vector nor the perpendicular distance from its line of action to the point or axis of interest changes. Therefore, the moment remains the same. This property is what makes it a "sliding vector".

This concept is fundamental in physics and engineering, as it helps us understand and calculate the effects of forces on the rotation of objects.

<p style="text-align: right;"><a href="#vectors">Back to top of chapter</a></p>
 
## 14. Moment of a Sliding Vector about a Line
The moment of a sliding vector about a line of interest l is the projection onto that line of the moment of the vector with respect to any point on the line.

![Figure 14.1: Moment of a sliding vector v about line l.](vector:svg1_9)

The magnitude of the moment of a sliding vector about a line g, $\|\vec M_l\|$, can be calculated using the scalar and vector product of three vectors:
1.  Unit vector along the line of interest g, $\vec u_l = (u_{lx}, u_{ly}, u_{lz})$.
2.  Position vector $\vec r = (r_x, r_y, r_z)$ from any point on the line of interest g to any point on the line of action of the sliding vector $\vec v$.
3.  The sliding vector $\vec v = (v_x, v_y, v_z)$.

That is:

$$
\|\vec M_l\| = \vec u_l \cdot (\vec r \times \vec v) \tag{14.1}
$$

To further simplify the calculations, we can mathematically combine the vector and scalar product into what is known as a **scalar triple product**. In the scalar triple product, we write the product as a determinant of the form:

$$
\|\vec M_l\| = \vec u_l \cdot (\vec r \times \vec v) = \begin{vmatrix}u_{lx} & u_{ly} & u_{lz} \\ r_x & r_y & r_z \\ v_x & v_y & v_z\\ \end{vmatrix} = u_{lx}\cdot\begin{vmatrix} r_y & r_z \\ v_y & v_z \\ \end{vmatrix} - u_{ly}\cdot\begin{vmatrix} r_x & r_z \\ v_x & v_z \\ \end{vmatrix} + u_{lz}\cdot\begin{vmatrix} r_x & r_y \\ v_x & v_y \\ \end{vmatrix} \tag{14.2}
$$

Finally, since a vector can be expressed as the product of its magnitude and its unit vector, we have:

$$
\vec M_l = \|\vec M_l\| \cdot \vec u_l \tag{14.3}
$$

## 15. Vector Functions
A **vector function** is a mathematical function of one or more variables whose range is a set of vectors.

For example, a common type of vector function is one that depends on a single real parameter t and produces a vector $\vec f(t)$ as a result. In terms of the unit vectors $\hat\imath$, $\hat\jmath$, $\hat k$ of the 3D Cartesian space, these specific types of vector-valued functions are given by expressions such as:

$$
\vec f(t) = f_x(t)\hat\imath + f_y(t)\hat\jmath + f_z(t)\hat k \tag{15.1}
$$

Where $f_x(t)$, $f_y(t)$, and $f_z(t)$ are the coordinate functions of the parameter t, and the domain of this vector function is the intersection of the domains of the functions $f_x(t)$, $f_y(t)$, and $f_z(t)$.

<p style="text-align: right;"><a href="#vectors">Back to top of chapter</a></p>

## 16. Derivative of a Vector Function
The **derivative of a vector function** is almost identical to the definition of the derivative of a real function of one variable. However, because the range of a vector-valued function consists of vectors, the same is true for the range of the derivative of a vector-valued function. That is:

$$
\vec f'(t) = \frac{d\vec f}{dt} = \frac{df_x}{dt}\hat\imath + \frac{df_y}{dt}\hat\jmath +\frac{df_z}{dt}\hat k \tag{16.1}
$$

<p style="text-align: right;"><a href="#vectors">Back to top of chapter</a></p>
 
## 17. Partial Derivative of a Function and its Total Derivative
If a function depends on more than one variable, its **partial derivative** is the derivative with respect to one of those variables while keeping the others constant.

The partial derivative of a function $f(x,y,z)$ with respect to one of its variables, x, is represented as:

$$
\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \tag{17.1}
$$

The **total derivative** of a function $f(x,y,z)$ can be calculated using the expression:

$$
df = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}dx + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}dy + \frac{\partial f}{\partial z}dz \tag{17.2}
$$

<p style="text-align: right;"><a href="#vectors">Back to top of chapter</a></p>
 
## 18. Integral of a Vector Function
The **integral of a vector function** is another vector function in which its coordinate functions are the integrals of the coordinate functions of the initial vector function. That is, if $\vec f(t) = f_x(t)\vec i + f_y(t)\vec j + f_z(t)\vec k = (f_x(t), f_y(t), f_z(t))$, its integral is:

$$
\int \vec f(t) dt = \int (f_x(t), f_y(t), f_z(t)) dt = \left( \int f_x(t)dt, \int f_y(t)dt, \int f_z(t)dt \right) \tag{18.1}
$$
