1. Theory and Key Concepts

1.1 Introduction

A body moves when over time it changes position relative to a reference point that is assumed to be fixed.

In this article, dedicated to kinematics, we study the movement of physical bodies without considering the causes that produce it. This objective will be achieved when we know at each instant where the moving body is located and how it moves. We must therefore obtain an equation that relates the position of the body to time.

To reduce complexity and focus on the basic concepts of kinematics, instead of studying the motion of any body, we will begin by studying the motion of a material point; with which we sometimes roughly represent a body. And we define it as a geometric point in which we consider that all the mass of the body is concentrated.

1.2 Movement and trajectory

To determine the position of a material point, it is necessary to specify the coordinate system relative to which its position is measured.

The position of a point, relative to the chosen reference system, is described by the position vector \(\vec r\).

To study the motion of the material point, we will analyze how the position vector \(\vec r\) varies over time. Thus, we will seek to determine the vector function \(\vec r(t)\), which describes how each of its components varies with time (t).

\( \vec r(t) = x(t)\vec i + y(t)\vec j + z(t)\vec k = (x(t), y(t), z(t)) \)
[1.1]

The end of the position vector \(\vec r\) draws a line in space described by the material point in its movement. This route or path that the moving point follows over time is called a trajectory. We can imagine the trajectory as a line that marks the exact path of the object.

If we know the trajectory of the moving point (for example, a straight line, a circle, etc.), then the problem of describing its motion is greatly simplified. All we need to find is an equation that tells us at what point of that trajectory the object is at each instant of time. If we call s the path traveled, the kinematic problem is therefore reduced to knowing s = f(t).

For example, imagine a ball falling in a straight line. The trajectory is a vertical line. If we find an equation s = f(t), which tells us how high the ball is at each second, then we have solved the kinematic problem for that ball.

1.3 Displacement Vector

The instant at which the study of the motion of a material point begins is called the initial instant \(t_0\). The position of the point at that instant is the initial position \(\vec r_0\).

Since the temporal reference system, like the spatial reference instant, is arbitrary, we consider \(t_0\) = 0.

As time goes by, the moving body will change position. At time \(t_1\), its position will be \(\vec r(t_1)\), at time \(t_2\), it will be \(\vec r(t_2)\) and so on.

So, to represent the change in position of the material point, we define the displacement vector \(\Delta \vec r\) in the time interval [\(t_1\), \(t_2\)] as a vector whose origin is the position of the point at the instant \(t_1\) and whose end is the position at the instant \(t_2\).

Thus,

\(\Delta\vec r = \vec r(t_2) - \vec r(t_1)\)
[1.2]

If we call \(\Delta t\) the elapsed time interval, we can write

  \(t_1 = t\)

  \(t_2 = t + \Delta t\)

\(\Delta\vec r = \vec r(t + \Delta t) - \vec r(t)\)
[1.3]

which can also be expressed as

\(\Delta\vec r = \Delta x\vec i + \Delta y\vec j + \Delta z\vec k\)
[1.4]

where \(\Delta x\), \(\Delta y\) and \(\Delta z\) represents how the coordinates of the point vary in the time interval considered.

If the body moves in a straight line, and without changing the direction of its movement, the magnitude of the displacement vector coincides with the distance traveled by the body. However, this only applies in these circumstances, because the displacement vector does not depend on the path followed by the object, only on the initial and final points.

1.4 Velocity

We said in the Introduction that in the study of the motion of a point we are interested in knowing where it is and how it moves at each instant. We achieve the first objective by knowing the temporal variation of the position vector \(\vec r\), and to know how it moves, that is, what will happen to the moving point in successive instants, we introduce a new magnitude called velocity.

Suppose that a certain material point P moves in a time interval \(\Delta t\) from a point 1 to a point 2. Characterized respectively by the position vectors \(\vec r_1\) and \(\vec r_2\).

The average velocity \(\vec v_{avg}\) in the time interval \(\Delta t\) is defined as:

$$ \vec v_{avg} = \frac{\Delta \vec r}{\Delta t} $$
[1.5]

The instantaneous velocity \(\vec v\) is defined as:

$$ \vec v = \lim_{\Delta t\to 0} \frac{\Delta \vec r}{\Delta t} = \frac{d \vec r}{d t} $$
[1.6]

That is, as the speed of change of the position vector \(\vec r\) with respect to time.

This vector is tangent to the path and its direction is that of movement.

If we refer the instantaneous velocity to the path traveled, or the length of the path, the instantaneous velocity can be expresed in the following way:

$$ \vec v = \lim_{\Delta t\to 0} \frac{\Delta \vec r}{\Delta t} = \lim_{\Delta t\to 0} \frac{\Delta \vec r}{\Delta s}\cdot \frac{\Delta s}{\Delta t} = \lim_{\Delta t\to 0} \frac{\Delta \vec r}{\Delta s}\cdot \lim_{\Delta t\to 0} \frac{\Delta s}{\Delta t} $$
[1.7]

To each displacement vector \(\Delta \vec r\) corresponds an interval of the path length \(\Delta s\), for each time interval \(\Delta t\). Normally, \(\Delta s > \vert \Delta \vec r \vert \), but when considering time intervals \(\Delta t\) that tend to zero, both values ​​tend to coincide. That is, if \(\Delta t \to 0\), the arc \(\Delta s \to \) the chord \(\vert \Delta \vec r \vert \). And the first limit will be a vector of unit module, tangent to the path and in the direction of the movement. Vector that we will symbolize by \(\vec \tau\).

The second limit is a scalar and represents the module of the instantaneous velocity \(\vert \vec r \vert\), it is called celerity and is represented by \(v\).

So,

$$ \vec v = v\cdot \vec\tau $$
[1.8]

If we refer the instantaneous velocity to the components of the position vector, we can express the instantaneous velocity as follows:

$$ \vec v = \frac {d\vec r}{dt} = \frac {dx}{dt}\vec i + \frac {dy}{dt}\vec j + \frac {dz}{dt}\vec k $$
[1.9]

That is,

$$ \vec v = v_x\vec i + v_y\vec j + v_z\vec k $$
[1.10]

Where the module of the instantaneous speed will be:

$$ v = \vert \vec v\vert = \sqrt{v_x^2+v_y^2+v_z^2} $$
[1.11]

1.5 Acceleration

Acceleration, in general, is the variation in speed over a time interval. Since speed is a vector magnitude, acceleration will be different from zero whenever the speed varies in its magnitude, direction or sense.

To determine the motion of a particle, it is necessary to know how the acceleration changes over time. To do this, we introduce the concepts of average acceleration and instantaneous acceleration.

The average acceleration represents how the speed varies in each unit of time, or the vector that results from dividing the speed variation in a given time interval by said interval.

$$ \vec a = \frac{\Delta\vec v}{\Delta t} = \frac{\Delta v_x}{\Delta t}\vec i + \frac{\Delta v_y}{\Delta t}\vec j + \frac{\Delta v_z}{\Delta t}\vec k $$
[1.12]

The instantaneous acceleration is the acceleration that the particle has at any instant \(t\), or at any point in the trajectory. Thus, we can express the instantaneous acceleration as the limit value of the average acceleration when the time interval tends to zero.

$$ \vec a = \lim_{\Delta t\to 0} \frac{\Delta \vec v}{\Delta t} = \frac{d \vec v}{d t} = \frac{d^2 \vec r}{d t^2} $$
[1.13]

The average acceleration has little practical value. Whereas instantaneous acceleration is of great importance. Therefore, from now on, we will always refer to instantaneous acceleration when we talk about acceleration.

Cartesian components of acceleration

If we know the function that expresses the position vector as a function of time \(\vec r = f(t)\), we can express the Cartesian components of acceleration as:

$$ \vec a = \frac{d \vec v}{d t} = \frac{d (v_x\vec i + v_y\vec j + v_z\vec k)}{d t} = \frac{dv_x}{dt}\vec i + \frac{dv_y}{dt}\vec j + \frac{dv_z}{dt}\vec k = a_x\vec i + a_y\vec j + a_z\vec k $$
[1.14]

The acceleration module will then be:

$$ \vert \vec a\vert = \sqrt{a_x^2+a_y^2+a_z^2} $$
[1.15]

Intrinsic components of acceleration

When analyzing the motion of an object along a curved path, it is very useful to describe its acceleration in terms of a reference system that adapts to the shape of that curve at every instant. This reference system is called intrinsic because it is linked to the path itself.

At each point of the trajectory we can define:

  • A point: This is simply the point of the trajectory we are considering at a given moment.
  • A tangent axis: This axis is a straight line that passes through the point and is parallel to the velocity of the object at that instant. It indicates the direction in which the object is moving.
  • A normal axis: This axis is a straight line perpendicular to the tangent axis at the considered point. It points towards the center of curvature of the trajectory.

Together, these three elements form a local coordinate system that moves and rotates as the object travels along the curve.

By decomposing the acceleration vector into these axes, we obtain two components:

  • Tangential acceleration (\(a_{\tau}\)): This component indicates the rate of change of the magnitude of the velocity. If it is positive, the object accelerates; if it is negative, the object decelerates.
  • Normal acceleration ((\(a_{\eta}\)): This component measures the rate of change of the direction of the velocity. It always points towards the center of curvature and is related to how quickly the object changes direction.

If we know the curve that defines the particle's trajectory and its path traveled as a function of time \(s=f(t)\), we can express the variation in speed \(\Delta \vec v\) between two points on the trajectory, \(P_1\) and \(P_2\), where the particle's speed is \(\vec v_1\) and \(\vec v_2\) respectively, by means of the composition of two vectors: one in the direction of \(\vec v_1\) and another perpendicular to \(\vec v_1\). Then, the variation in speed would be:

$$ \Delta \vec v = \Delta\vec v_{\tau} + \Delta\vec v_{\mu} $$
[1.16]

The average acceleration would be:

$$ \frac{\Delta \vec v}{\Delta t} = \frac{\Delta\vec v_{\tau}}{\Delta t} + \frac{\Delta\vec v_{\mu}}{\Delta t} $$
[1.17]

And the instantaneous acceleration would be:

$$ \vec a = \lim_{\Delta t\to 0} \frac{\Delta \vec v}{\Delta t} = \lim_{\Delta t\to 0}\frac{\Delta\vec v_{\tau}}{\Delta t} + \lim_{\Delta t\to 0}\frac{\Delta\vec v_{\mu}}{\Delta t} = \vec a_{\tau} + \vec a_{\mu} = a_{\tau}\vec {\tau} + a_{\mu}\vec {\mu} $$
[1.18]

Then, the instantaneous acceleration can be expressed by the intrinsic components of acceleration as the sum of two accelerations, one in the direction tangent to the trajectory (tangential acceleration) and the other in the direction normal to the trajectory (normal acceleration) at each point of said trajectory.

Let us now calculate the magnitudes of the tangential acceleration and the normal acceleration. By differentiating the expression of velocity based on the trajectory [1.8]:

$$ \vec a = \frac{d \vec v}{d t} = \frac{d (v\cdot \vec\tau)}{d t} = \frac{d v}{d t}\cdot \vec\tau + v \cdot \frac{d \vec\tau}{d t} $$
[1.19]

Relating [1.18] and [1.19], it can be observed that the first term of 1.19 \(\frac{d v}{d t}\cdot \vec\tau\) is the tangential acceleration. Thus, the magnitude of the tangential acceleration is simply the time derivative of the magnitude of the velocity (speed):

$$ a_{\tau} = \left \vert \frac{\Delta \vec v}{\Delta t} \right \vert $$
[1.20]

Similarly, the second term of [1.19] \(v \cdot \frac{d \vec\tau}{d t}\) corresponds to the normal acceleration; it represents the rate of change of the direction of the velocity and is directed in the same direction as the normal unit vector \( \vec\eta \).

$$ v \cdot \frac{d \vec\tau}{d t} = v \cdot \frac{d (\vert \vec\tau\vert\cdot\vec\eta)}{d t} = v \cdot \frac{\vert d\vec\tau\vert}{d t}\cdot\vec\eta $$
[1.21]

Let us consider a small displacement along the trajectory; so small that the path of this displacement approximates a circular arc with a certain radius R. Therefore, during this displacement, the object travels along a circular arc of radius R (called the radius of curvature) and subtends an angle \( d\theta \) radians.

From the definition of a radian (a radian is the central angle of a circle that subtends an arc equal in length to the radius of the circle), we find that \( d s = R \cdot d\theta \).

For small angles, we can approximate the change in the tangent unit vector \(\vec\tau\) to the circular arc described by the tip of the vector as it rotates. The length of this arc is approximately equal to the radius of the circle (which is 1, since we are talking about a unit vector) multiplied by the angle \( d \theta \). Therefore, for small angles, we can write \( \vert d\vec\tau \vert \approx d\theta \). Then:

$$ \left. \begin{align} d s = R \cdot d\theta \\ \vert d\vec\tau \vert \approx d\theta \end{align} \right \} \implies d s = \vert d\vec\tau \vert \cdot R \implies \frac{\vert d\vec\tau \vert}{d s} = { 1 \over R} $$

Thus, rewriting [1.19]:

$$ \vec a = \frac{d \vec v}{d t} = \frac{d v}{d t}\cdot \vec\tau + v \cdot \frac{\vert d\vec\tau\vert}{d t}\cdot\vec\eta = \frac{d v}{d t}\cdot \vec\tau + v \cdot \frac{d\tau}{d s}\cdot \frac{d s}{d t}\cdot \vec\eta = \frac{d v}{d t}\cdot \vec\tau + {v^2 \over R}\cdot \vec\eta $$
[1.22]

Finally, comparing with [1.18], we find that the intrinsic coordinates of acceleration are:

1. Tangential Acceleration (\(a_{\tau}\)):

This component is responsible for the change in the magnitude of the velocity (i.e., the change in speed). It is parallel to the direction of motion at every instant. Its expression is defined as:

$$ a_{\tau} = \frac{d v}{d t} $$
[1.23]

where \(v\) is the magnitude of the velocity (speed) and \(t\) is time. If the particle speeds up, \(a_{\tau}\) will be positive; if it slows down, it will be negative.

2. Normal Acceleration (\(a_{\eta}\)):

This component is responsible for the change in the direction of the velocity. It is always perpendicular to the trajectory and points toward the center of curvature of the path at that point. Its expression is defined as:

$$ a_{\eta} = \frac{v^2}{R} $$
[1.24]

where \(v\) is the magnitude of the velocity (speed) and \(R\) is the radius of curvature of the trajectory at that point. This component is always non-negative, as a change in direction is always required for curved motion.

We can say that intrinsic coordinates offer an intuitive view of how acceleration affects both the particle's speed and the shape of its trajectory.

1.6 Kinematics of some types of Motion

Let us apply the concepts presented so far to some of the most important types of motion:

  • Uniform Rectilinear Motion (URM).
  • Uniformly Accelerated Rectilinear Motion (UARM).
  • Uniform Circular Motion (UCM).
  • Uniformly Accelerated Circular Motion (UACM).

1.6.1 Uniform Rectilinear Motion (URM)

A motion is rectilinear and uniform when its velocity is constant. This implies:

  • The velocity is constant in direction and sense: the trajectory is a straight line.
  • The velocity is constant in magnitude: it covers equal distances in equal time intervals.

It can also be defined in terms of acceleration by stating: Uniform Rectilinear Motion (URM) is one that has no acceleration. This implies:

\[ a_{\tau} = \frac{dv}{dt} = 0 \implies v = \text{constant} \] \[ a_{\eta} = \frac{v^2}{R} = 0 \implies R = \infty \quad \text{The trajectory is a straight line.} \]

To derive the law governing this motion, we start from the definition of instantaneous velocity [1.6]:

\[ \vec v = \frac{d \vec r}{dt} \implies d \vec r = \vec v dt \]

To solve for the position vector, we integrate the previous equation:

\[ \vec r (t) = \int \vec v dt = \vec v \int dt = \vec v t + C \]

This function must hold for any value of \(t\). Therefore, it must also hold for \(t = 0\):

\[ \left. \begin{matrix} \vec{r} = \vec v t + C \\ t = 0 \end{matrix} \right\} \implies \vec{r}_0 = C \]

The integration constant is the initial position \(\vec{r}_0\), and therefore, the vector equation of URM will be:

$$\vec r (t) = \vec{r}_0 + \vec v t$$
[1.25]

For this type of motion, we can choose a one-dimensional reference system, where the position of the particle along the straight line can be defined simply by a scalar value \(x\), making one of the axes—the \(x\)-axis, in this case—coincide with the direction of motion: \( \vec r = (x, 0, 0)\).

Thus, the previous equation reduces to a scalar equation:

\[ x(t) = x_0 + vt \quad \text{position equation.} \]

Therefore, for URM, we express displacement as a scalar, whose value coincides with the distance traveled:

\[ \left. \begin{matrix} \Delta x = x(t_2) - x(t_1) = v\Delta t \\ \Delta x = \Delta s \end{matrix} \right \} \implies \Delta s = v\Delta t \]

Similarly, for this type of motion, we can represent velocity using a scalar. Thus, the average velocity is:

\[ v = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \frac{\Delta s}{\Delta t} \]

By establishing a one-dimensional reference system, it is extremely useful to employ an x-t diagram to represent the motion. This diagram is, by definition, the graphical representation of the function \(x = f(t)\) describing the motion.

For uniform rectilinear motion, where the position is given by \(x = x_0 + vt\), the x-t diagram is visualized as a straight line. In this representation, the y-intercept (the point where the line crosses the vertical axis) corresponds to the initial position of the particle (\(x_0\)), and the slope of this line directly indicates the velocity of the particle.

In the URM x-t diagram, the slope (\(\tan \alpha\)) of the line describing the motion, calculated over a specific time interval, is obtained by dividing the change in the vertical coordinate (the particle's position) by the change in the horizontal coordinate (the elapsed time). In the x-t diagram, this corresponds directly to the average velocity of the motion during that interval:

$$ \tan \alpha = \frac{x(t_1) - x_0}{t_1} = v_{\text{avg}}$$
[1.26]
Table 1.1: Summary of URM equations.
Magnitude Equation
Position vector: \( \vec{r}(t) = \vec{r}_0 + \vec{v} \; t \)
Position (scalar): \( x(t) = x_0 + v t \)
Velocity: \( v = \text{constant} \)
Displacement: \( \Delta x = v \Delta t,\; \Delta s = v \Delta t \)
x-t Diagram: Slope \( = v \) (straight line: \( x = x_0 + v t \))

1.6.2 Uniformly Accelerated Rectilinear Motion (UARM)

A motion is called uniformly accelerated rectilinear motion (UARM) when it has no normal acceleration and its tangential acceleration is constant.

Since it has no normal acceleration, the trajectory of this motion describes a straight line. Therefore, if we choose the direction of motion as our reference system, as we did for uniform rectilinear motion, all vector magnitudes become scalars.

Starting from the definition of tangential acceleration and integrating, we obtain the velocity equation at any instant:

\[ a = \frac{dv}{dt} \Rightarrow dv = a \, dt \Rightarrow v(t) = \int a \, dt = a \int dt = at + C \]

This function must hold for any value of \(t\). Therefore, it must also hold for \(t = 0\).

\[ \left. \begin{matrix} v(t) = at + C \\ t = 0 \end{matrix} \right\} \implies v_0 = C \]

The integration constant represents the initial velocity.

The equation of motion that allows calculating the velocity at any instant is:

\[ v(t) = v_0 + at \]
[1.27]

To find the position equation at any instant, we start from the definition of velocity and integrate:

\[ v = \frac{dx}{dt} \Rightarrow dx = v \, dt \Rightarrow x(t) = \int v \, dt = \int (v_0 + at) \, dt \\ = \int v_0 \, dt + \int at \, dt = v_0 \int dt + a \int t \, dt = v_0 t + \frac{1}{2} at^2 + C \]

If this function must hold for any value of time, the integration constant represents the initial position \(C = x_0\).

The scalar equation of UARM, which gives us the position at any instant, will be:

\[ x(t) = x_0 + v_0 t + \frac{1}{2} at^2 \]
[1.28]

To represent UARM on an x-t diagram, we plot the function \(x=f(t)\), where the position is given by [1.28]. The x-t diagram is visualized as a parabola, whose y-intercept corresponds to the initial position of the moving object (\(x_0\)).

Unlike URM where velocity is constant, in UARM the velocity varies continuously. To understand the velocity at a specific instant, we turn to the concept of instantaneous velocity. Similar to how the tangent in URM represents constant velocity, in UARM, where the position vs. time graph (\(x\) vs. \(t\)) is a curve (a parabola), the instantaneous velocity at each point of the curve corresponds to the slope of the tangent line to the curve at that instant. This instantaneous slope is, by definition, the derivative of position with respect to time:

$$ \tan \alpha = \frac{dx}{dt} = v_{\text{instantaneous}} $$
[1.29]

Summary of UARM equations

Magnitude Equation
Acceleration: \( a = \text{constant} \)
Velocity: \( v(t) = v_0 + a t \)
Position: \( x(t) = x_0 + v_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2} a t^2 \)

1.6.3 Uniform Circular Motion (UCM)

Uniform Circular Motion (UCM) describes the motion of an object traveling in a circular path at a constant speed. To understand how we derive the equation that defines its position at any given time, we begin with the key characteristics of its acceleration.

Accelerations in UCM

As we already know, the acceleration of a moving object can be decomposed into two intrinsic components: tangential acceleration (\(a_t\)) and normal acceleration (or centripetal acceleration, \(a_n\)).

  • Tangential Acceleration (\(a_t\)): This component of acceleration acts in the direction of motion (tangent to the trajectory) and is responsible for changing the magnitude of velocity (the speed) of the object. In UCM, the speed is, by definition, constant. Therefore, there is no change in the magnitude of velocity, which means that the tangential acceleration is zero (\(a_t = 0\)).
  • Normal or Centripetal Acceleration (\(a_n\)): This component acts perpendicular to the velocity and always points toward the center of curvature of the trajectory. It is responsible for changing the direction of velocity. For an object to move in a circle, the direction of its velocity must change continuously. In UCM, the trajectory is a circle of radius \(R\), and the magnitude of the normal acceleration remains constant, given by the formula:
    \[ a_n = \frac{v^2}{R} \]
    [1.30]
    Since speed (\(v\)) and radius (\(R\)) are constant in UCM, the magnitude of the normal acceleration (\(a_n\)) is also constant.

Particle Position: Introduction to Polar Coordinates and the Radian

To describe the position of a particle on a circular trajectory, the polar coordinate system \((r, \theta)\) is the most natural and convenient.

  • The Radius (\(r\)): For a circular path centered at the origin, the radius \(r\) is simply the constant radius of the circle, which we denote as \(R\). Thus, the first polar coordinate is \(r=R\).
  • The Angle (\(\theta\)): The second polar coordinate is the angle \(\theta\), representing the angular position of the particle relative to a reference axis (usually the positive \(x\)-axis). This angle is measured in radians.

Definition of Radian: A radian is the unit of measure for an angle that subtends an arc equal in length to the radius of a circle. If the arc of a circle measures \(s\) and the radius of the circle is \(R\), then the angle \(\theta\) (in radians) subtended by that arc is:

\[ \theta = \frac{s}{R} \]
[1.31]

From this definition, we can deduce a fundamental relationship: the arc length (\(s\)) on a circle is equal to the radius (\(R\)) multiplied by the angle (\(\theta\)) subtended by that arc (measured in radians). For an infinitesimal change in angular position \(d\theta\), the infinitesimal arc length \(ds\) is:

\[ ds = R d\theta \]

This relationship connects the distance traveled along the trajectory (a linear quantity) with the change in angular position (an angular quantity).

Angular Velocity (\(\omega\)) as a Constant in UCM

Given that the tangential acceleration is zero (\(a_t=0\)), we confirm that the linear speed (\(v = ds/dt\)) is constant. Using the relationship \(ds = R d\theta\), we can express the linear speed as:

\[ v = \frac{ds}{dt} = \frac{R d\theta}{dt} = R \frac{d\theta}{dt} \]

The quantity \(\frac{d\theta}{dt}\) is the angular velocity (\(\omega\)), which represents how fast the angle \(\theta\) changes with time. Therefore, we have:

\[ v = \omega R \]
[1.32]

Since \(v\) is constant and \(R\) (the radius of the circle) is also constant, it follows that the angular velocity (\(\omega\)) must also be constant for UCM. This constant \(\omega\) (measured in radians per second) is the cornerstone for describing position over time.

Equation of Motion for Angular Position and Integration

The definition of angular velocity as the rate of change of angular position gives us the following differential equation:

\[ \omega = \frac{d\theta}{dt} \]
[1.33]

Since \(\omega\) is constant in UCM, we can solve this simple differential equation through integration. We multiply by \(dt\) to separate the variables:

\[ d\theta = \omega dt \]

Now, we integrate both sides. If at the initial instant \(t=0\) the angular position is \(\theta_0\), and at an instant \(t\) the angular position is \(\theta(t)\):

\[ \int_{\theta_0}^{\theta(t)} d\theta = \int_{0}^{t} \omega dt \] \[ [\theta]_{\theta_0}^{\theta(t)} = \omega [t]_{0}^{t} \] \[ \theta(t) - \theta_0 = \omega (t - 0) \]
\[ \theta(t) = \omega t + \theta_0 \]
[1.34]

Equation [1.34] gives us the angular position of the particle at any instant of time \(t\). Thus, the position of the particle in polar coordinates at any instant \(t\) is \((R, \omega t + \theta_0)\).

Transformation to Cartesian Coordinates: The Position Vector

Finally, to express the particle's position in a Cartesian coordinate system \((x, y)\), we use the trigonometric relationships connecting polar and Cartesian coordinates:

\[ x = r \cos(\theta) \] \[ y = r \sin(\theta) \]

Substituting \(r=R\) and the expression for \(\theta(t)\) we just derived:

\[ x(t) = R \cos(\omega t + \theta_0) \] \[ y(t) = R \sin(\omega t + \theta_0) \]

The position vector \(\vec{r}(t)\) is built using the unit vectors \(\hat{i}\) (for the \(x\) direction) and \(\hat{j}\) (for the \(y\) direction):

\[ \vec{r}(t) = x(t) \hat{i} + y(t) \hat{j} \]

By substituting the expressions for \(x(t)\) and \(y(t)\), we obtain the position vector for UCM:

\[ \vec{r}(t) = (R \cos(\omega t + \theta_0)) \hat{i} + (R \sin(\omega t + \theta_0)) \hat{j} \]
[1.35]

Equation [1.35] allows us to know the exact location of an object in UCM at any instant of time, based on its radius, constant angular velocity, and initial angular position.

The Velocity Vector in Cartesian Coordinates

Once we have the position vector \(\vec{r}(t)\), we can obtain the velocity vector \(\vec{v}(t)\) by differentiating \(\vec{r}(t)\) with respect to time:

\[ \vec{v}(t) = \frac{d\vec{r}(t)}{dt} = \frac{d}{dt} \left[ (R \cos(\omega t + \theta_0)) \hat{i} + (R \sin(\omega t + \theta_0)) \hat{j} \right] \]

Differentiating component by component:

\[ v_x(t) = \frac{d}{dt} (R \cos(\omega t + \theta_0)) = -R \sin(\omega t + \theta_0) \cdot \omega = -R\omega \sin(\omega t + \theta_0) \] \[ v_y(t) = \frac{d}{dt} (R \sin(\omega t + \theta_0)) = R \cos(\omega t + \theta_0) \cdot \omega = R\omega \cos(\omega t + \theta_0) \]

Therefore, the velocity vector is:

\[ \vec{v}(t) = (-R\omega \sin(\omega t + \theta_0)) \hat{i} + (R\omega \cos(\omega t + \theta_0)) \hat{j} \]

Now, let us calculate the magnitude of this velocity vector to verify it matches the constant speed \(v = R\omega\):

\[ \|\vec{v}(t)\| = \sqrt{v_x(t)^2 + v_y(t)^2} \] \[ \|\vec{v}(t)\| = \sqrt{(-R\omega \sin(\omega t + \theta_0))^2 + (R\omega \cos(\omega t + \theta_0))^2} \] \[ \|\vec{v}(t)\| = \sqrt{R^2\omega^2 \sin^2(\omega t + \theta_0) + R^2\omega^2 \cos^2(\omega t + \theta_0)} \] \[ \|\vec{v}(t)\| = \sqrt{R^2\omega^2 (\sin^2(\omega t + \theta_0) + \cos^2(\omega t + \theta_0))} \]

Since \(\sin^2(x) + \cos^2(x) = 1\):

\[ \|\vec{v}(t)\| = \sqrt{R^2\omega^2 \cdot 1} = \sqrt{R^2\omega^2} = |R\omega| \]

As \(R\) and \(\omega\) are positive magnitudes, the magnitude of the velocity is:

\[ \|\vec{v}(t)\| = R\omega \]
[1.36]

This confirms that the magnitude of the velocity vector is the constant speed \(R\omega\), as expected for UCM.

The Acceleration Vector in Cartesian Coordinates

To obtain the acceleration vector \(\vec{a}(t)\), we differentiate the velocity vector \(\vec{v}(t)\) with respect to time:

\[ \vec{a}(t) = \frac{d\vec{v}(t)}{dt} = \frac{d}{dt} \left[ (-R\omega \sin(\omega t + \theta_0)) \hat{i} + (R\omega \cos(\omega t + \theta_0)) \hat{j} \right] \]

Differentiating component by component:

\[ a_x(t) = \frac{d}{dt} (-R\omega \sin(\omega t + \theta_0)) = -R\omega \cos(\omega t + \theta_0) \cdot \omega = -R\omega^2 \cos(\omega t + \theta_0) \] \[ a_y(t) = \frac{d}{dt} (R\omega \cos(\omega t + \theta_0)) = -R\omega \sin(\omega t + \theta_0) \cdot \omega = -R\omega^2 \sin(\omega t + \theta_0) \]

Therefore, the acceleration vector is:

\[ \vec{a}(t) = (-R\omega^2 \cos(\omega t + \theta_0)) \hat{i} + (-R\omega^2 \sin(\omega t + \theta_0)) \hat{j} \]

We can factor out \(-R\omega^2\) from the expression:

\[ \vec{a}(t) = -R\omega^2 [ (\cos(\omega t + \theta_0)) \hat{i} + (\sin(\omega t + \theta_0)) \hat{j} ] \]

Notice that the expression in brackets is exactly the position vector \(\vec{r}(t)\) divided by \(R\), which is the unit vector in the radial direction, \(\hat{u}_r = \frac{\vec{r}(t)}{R}\). This means the acceleration always points in the opposite direction of the position vector (toward the center of the circle), which is consistent with centripetal acceleration.

\[ \vec{a}(t) = -\omega^2 \vec{r}(t) \]

Finally, let us calculate the magnitude of the acceleration vector:

\[ \|\vec{a}(t)\| = \sqrt{a_x(t)^2 + a_y(t)^2} \] \[ \|\vec{a}(t)\| = \sqrt{(-R\omega^2 \cos(\omega t + \theta_0))^2 + (-R\omega^2 \sin(\omega t + \theta_0))^2} \] \[ \|\vec{a}(t)\| = \sqrt{R^2\omega^4 \cos^2(\omega t + \theta_0) + R^2\omega^4 \sin^2(\omega t + \theta_0)} \] \[ \|\vec{a}(t)\| = \sqrt{R^2\omega^4 (\cos^2(\omega t + \theta_0) + \sin^2(\omega t + \theta_0))} \] \[ \|\vec{a}(t)\| = \sqrt{R^2\omega^4 \cdot 1} = \sqrt{R^2\omega^4} = |R\omega^2| \]

Since \(R\) and \(\omega\) are positive, the magnitude of the acceleration is:

\[ \|\vec{a}(t)\| = R\omega^2 \]
[1.37]

Recalling that linear speed \(v = R\omega\), we can substitute \(\omega = v/R\) into the acceleration expression:

\[ \|\vec{a}(t)\| = R \left(\frac{v}{R}\right)^2 = R \frac{v^2}{R^2} = \frac{v^2}{R} \]
[1.38]

This confirms that the magnitude of the acceleration vector is precisely the centripetal acceleration (\(a_n\)) we defined at the beginning, which is consistent with the absence of tangential acceleration in UCM. The total acceleration is purely centripetal, responsible only for changing the direction of the velocity vector.

1.6.4 Uniformly Accelerated Circular Motion (UACM)

UACM is a motion along a circular path in which the angular acceleration remains constant over time (\(\alpha = \text{const} \neq 0\)). This implies that the angular velocity \(\omega\) increases or decreases uniformly.

Behavior of Intrinsic Components

Since we already know the vector nature of acceleration, in UACM its components behave as follows:

  • Tangential Acceleration (\( a_t \)): It is constant. Since \( \alpha \) is constant and the radius \( R \) is fixed, the rate at which the magnitude of the velocity varies is uniform: $$a_t = \alpha \cdot R = \text{const}$$
  • Normal Acceleration (\( a_n \)): It is variable. Although \( R \) is constant, the angular velocity \( \omega \) changes over time. Therefore, the acceleration required to curve the trajectory increases with the square of the velocity: $$a_n(t) = \omega(t)^2 \cdot R$$

Derivation of Equations by Integration

To obtain the equations of motion, we start from the linear relationship between tangential and angular acceleration: \( \alpha = a_t / R \). Since \( a_t \) is constant, \( \alpha \) is also constant.

1. Angular Velocity Equation (\( \omega \)):
We define angular acceleration as the derivative of angular velocity with respect to time and integrate:

$$\alpha = \frac{d\omega}{dt} \implies \int_{\omega_0}^{\omega} d\omega = \alpha \int_{0}^{t} dt$$
\[ \omega(t) = \omega_0 + \alpha t \]
[1.39]

2. Angular Position Equation (\( \varphi \)):
We define angular velocity as the derivative of position and integrate the expression obtained previously:

$$\omega = \frac{d\varphi}{dt} \implies d\varphi = (\omega_0 + \alpha t) dt$$ $$\int_{\varphi_0}^{\varphi} d\varphi = \int_{0}^{t} (\omega_0 + \alpha t) dt$$
\[ \varphi(t) = \varphi_0 + \omega_0 t + \frac{1}{2} \alpha t^2 \]
[1.40]

Time-Independent Relationship

To relate the angular velocity to the angle rotated without depending on time, we apply the chain rule to the angular acceleration:

$$\alpha = \frac{d\omega}{dt} = \frac{d\omega}{d\varphi} \cdot \frac{d\varphi}{dt} = \omega \frac{d\omega}{d\varphi}$$

Separating variables and integrating, we obtain:

$$\alpha \int_{\varphi_0}^{\varphi} d\varphi = \int_{\omega_0}^{\omega} \omega \, d\omega \implies \alpha \Delta\varphi = \frac{1}{2}\omega^2 - \frac{1}{2}\omega_0^2$$
\[ \omega^2 - \omega_0^2 = 2 \alpha \Delta\varphi \]
[1.41]

1.7 Composition of Motions

Any complex motion can be decomposed into three independent motions located along the Cartesian axes. Similarly, the composition of independent motions results in a single resultant motion.

The Principle of Superposition

The generalization of this idea constitutes the principle of superposition, which states that if a particle is simultaneously subjected to several independent elementary motions, the resulting motion is obtained by vectorially adding these partial motions.

For the practical application of this principle, the following vector magnitudes must be added separately:

  • Positions.
  • Displacements.
  • Velocities.
  • Accelerations.

Galileo's Principle of Independence

The principle of superposition is based on Galileo's principle of independence. This postulates that when a point is endowed with two simultaneous motions, its change in position is independent of whether both motions act successively or simultaneously.

Practical Applications

This principle allows for the resolution of many specific kinematic problems, such as:

  • The study of a swimmer crossing a river while being carried by the current.
  • The analysis of the motion of an object dropped by a passenger inside a moving vehicle (train, elevator, etc.).
  • The trajectory of a ball thrown vertically that is deflected by the wind.

1.8 Projectile Motion (Parabolic Motion)

Projectile motion is a fundamental practical case of the composition of motions. It occurs when we launch an object with an initial velocity \( \vec{v}_0 \) that forms an angle \( \theta \) with respect to the horizontal. By neglecting air resistance, the resulting motion is the superposition of two independent motions:

  • On the horizontal axis (X-axis): A Uniform Rectilinear Motion (URM), since no acceleration exists in this direction.
  • On the vertical axis (Y-axis): A Uniformly Accelerated Rectilinear Motion (UARM), subject to the acceleration of gravity \( g \approx 9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2 \), which always points toward the center of the Earth.

Decomposition of Initial Velocity

Starting from the polar coordinates of the initial velocity, we obtain its Cartesian components:

  • \( v_{0x} = v_0 \cdot \cos(\theta) \)
  • \( v_{0y} = v_0 \cdot \sin(\theta) \)

Equations of Motion

Applying the principle of superposition, we vectorially add the positions and velocities of both axes:

1. Velocity Equations:

On the X-axis, the velocity remains constant, while on the Y-axis, it varies uniformly:

  • \( v_x(t) = v_{0x} = v_0 \cdot \cos(\theta) \)
  • \( v_y(t) = v_{0y} - g \cdot t = v_0 \cdot \sin(\theta) - g \cdot t \)

2. Position Equations:

Integrating the velocity expressions with respect to time (considering the origin at ground level, \( x_0=0, y_0=0 \)):

  • X-axis (Horizontal): \( x(t) = v_{0x} \cdot t = (v_0 \cdot \cos\theta) \cdot t \)
  • Y-axis (Vertical): \( y(t) = v_{0y} \cdot t - \frac{1}{2}g \cdot t^2 = (v_0 \cdot \sin\theta) \cdot t - \frac{1}{2}g \cdot t^2 \)

Trajectory Equation

If we solve for time \( t \) in the horizontal position equation and substitute it into the vertical one, we obtain the relationship \( y = f(x) \):

$$y(x) = \tan(\theta) \cdot x - \frac{g}{2 v_0^2 \cos^2(\theta)} \cdot x^2$$

This expression mathematically corresponds to a parabola, confirming the geometry of this compound motion.

Maximum Height and Horizontal Range

To complete the study of projectile motion, it is essential to calculate two parameters that define the geometry of the trajectory: the maximum height and the horizontal range.

A) Maximum Height (\( H_{max} \))

It is reached when the projectile exhausts its vertical momentum and begins to descend. At this peak point, the vertical component of the velocity is zero: \( v_y = 0 \).

1. We use the velocity equation for the Y-axis to find the time of ascent (\( t_s \)):

\[ 0 = v_0 \sin\theta - g t_s \implies t_s = \frac{v_0 \sin\theta}{g} \]

2. We substitute this time into the vertical position equation (\( y \)):

\[ H_{max} = (v_0 \sin\theta) \left( \frac{v_0 \sin\theta}{g} \right) - \frac{1}{2}g \left( \frac{v_0 \sin\theta}{g} \right)^2 \]

3. After simplifying the terms, we obtain the final expression:

\[ H_{max} = \frac{v_0^2 \sin^2\theta}{2g} \]
[1.42]
B) Horizontal Range (\( R \))

It is the total distance traveled along the X-axis when the object returns to the launch level (\( y = 0 \)).

1. In a symmetrical launch, the time of flight (\( t_v \)) is twice the time of ascent:

\[ t_v = 2 t_s = \frac{2 v_0 \sin\theta}{g} \]

2. We substitute the time of flight into the horizontal position equation (\( x \)):

\[ R = (v_0 \cos\theta) \left( \frac{2 v_0 \sin\theta}{g} \right) = \frac{v_0^2 (2 \sin\theta \cos\theta)}{g} \]

3. Applying the double-angle trigonometric identity (\( 2 \sin\theta \cos\theta = \sin(2\theta) \)), we arrive at:

\[ R = \frac{v_0^2 \sin(2\theta)}{g} \]
[1.43]

Analysis: The maximum range is achieved when \( \sin(2\theta) = 1 \). This occurs when \( 2\theta = 90^\circ \), that is, for a launch angle of 45°.
