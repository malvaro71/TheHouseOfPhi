1.1 Theory and Key Concepts

1.1.1 Introduction

Have you ever wondered how physicists describe the motion of a planet, the flight of a ball, or the trajectory of a rocket? The key lies in a fundamental concept: position. Knowing "where" something is in the universe, at a specific moment and relative to a reference point, is the starting point.

Position is not just a piece of data; it is the foundation upon which pillars of physics such as displacement, velocity, and acceleration are built. Without it, it would be impossible to understand if an object has moved, in what direction, or how fast. But its importance goes beyond motion: it directly influences potential energy, how forces are applied and felt, and the description of gravitational, electric, or magnetic fields.

To handle this information accurately, physics relies on an indispensable mathematical tool: position vectors. These not only tell us how far an object is from an origin but also in what direction and sense it is located. They are the key to representing locations in two or three dimensions in a complete and rigorous way.

In this first chapter, we will explore vectors and the Cartesian coordinate system in depth, unraveling fundamental vector operations such as addition, subtraction, dot product, and cross product. These tools of vector algebra not only greatly simplify the resolution of complex problems in physics and engineering but also allow us, for example, to calculate the precise distance between two points with astounding mathematical elegance. Get ready to discover the language that allows us to describe and predict the physical world with unprecedented clarity.

1.1.2 Vectors

A vectoris a directed segment, one end of which (the point A) is said to be the origin, while the other (the point B) is said to be the end of the vector. Such a vector may be denoted by v, \(\vec v\) or \(\overrightarrow{AB}\). A vector whose origin and end coincide is said to be a zero vector, and is usually denoted by \(\vec0\). The vector \(\overrightarrow{BA}\) is said to be the vector opposite to \(\overrightarrow{AB}\). A vector with length equal to one is called a unit vector. Any direction may be assigned to a zero vector.

The vector origin is also named initial point or point of application.

A geometric vector, as defined above, is also called a fixed vector or bound vector. Because it has a unique initial point. In physics is used, for example, to specify the action of the force on deformable or non rigid body. In this instance, the forces and deformations within the body depend on the point of application of the force, as well as on the magnitude and line of action.

A class of equal vectors lying on the same straight line is said to be a sliding vector. An example of sliding vector is the action of a force on a rigid body. So, in this case, the point of application can be moved along the line determined by the vector direction.

If vectors are only characterized by magnitude and direction, then are called free vectors. Examples of free vectors in physics are displacement, velocity and magnetic or electric field strength. In these cases, the vector can be moved to any point in the space

Vector calculus, which is the study of operations performed on vectors, is based on free vectors, since two given free vectors are equivalent to a given sliding vector or a given bound vector, as long as we keep in mind the physical meaning of the vector under analysis. From now on, we'll talk about free vectors if not mentioned otherwise.

A vector is characterized:

  • by its magnitude (or module), which is equal to the length of the segment \(\overline{AB}\) and is denoted by \(\Vert\vec v\Vert\),
  • and by its direction: from A to B.

1.1.3 Vector Addition, Graphical Method:

The sum of two vectors \(\vec a\) and \(\vec b\), or vector addition, produces a third vector \(\overrightarrow{a+b}\).

To find \(\overrightarrow{a+b}\), we first draw the vector \(vec a\), and from the terminal end of \(vec a\), we draw the vector \(vec b\). In other words, we have the initial point of \(vec b\) meet the terminal end of \(vec a\). This position corresponds to the notion that we move along the first vector and then, from its terminal point, we move along the second vector. Vector addition has the conmutative property; so, if we first draw vector \(vec b\), and from the terminal end of \(vec b\), we draw the vector \(vec a\), we will obtain \(\overrightarrow{b+a}\), that is equal to \(\overrightarrow{a+b}\).

A scalar is a quantity that has magnitude but no direction. Or, in other words, a quantity that can be expressed by one (real) number.

1.1.4 Cartesian coordinate System

To specify the magnitude and direction of a vector we need a coordinate system. There are three commonly used coordinate systems: Cartesian, cylindrical and spherical. In this section we describe the Cartesian coordinate system.

A Cartesian coordinate system in two dimensions is defined by an ordered pair of perpendicular lines (axes), a single unit of length for both axes, and an orientation for each axis. The point where the axes meet is taken as the origin for both, thus turning each axis into a number line.

For any point P, a line is drawn through P perpendicular to each axis, and the position where it meetsthe axis is interpreted as a number. The two numbers, in that chosen order, are the Cartesian coordinates (x, y) of P.

A Cartesian coordinate system in three dimensions consists of three perpendicular axes: the x-axis, the y-axis, and the z-axis that go through a common point (the origin 0). Because each axis is a number line representing all real numbers in \(\Bbb R\) , the three-dimensional system is often denoted by \(\Bbb R^3\).

The cartesian coordinate system is also called a rectangular coordinate system or orthogonal coordinate system.

In Figure 1.4(a), the positive z-axis is shown above the plane containing the x- and y-axes. The system displayed follows the right-hand rule. If we take our right hand and align the fingers with the positive x-axis, then curl the fingers so they point in the direction of the positive y-axis, our thumb points in the direction of the positive z-axis. In this website, we always work with coordinate systems set up in accordance with the right-hand rule. Some systems do follow a left-hand rule, but the right-hand rule is considered the standard representation.

We describe a point P in the three-dimensional rectangular coordinate system with the coordinates \((x_1, y_1, z_1)\). Each coordinate describes how the point aligns with the corresponding axis.

1.1.5 Vector components

When the initial point of a vector coincides with the origin of the coordinate system, we call the vector a standard-position vector. Because the initial point of any vector in standard position is known to be (0,0), we can describe the vector by looking only at the coordinates of its terminal point. Thus, if vector \(vec v\) has its initial point at the origin and its terminal point at (x, y), we write the vector in component form as

\begin{flalign} \vec v = (x, y) \label{eq:5_1} \end{flalign}

When a vector is written in component form like this, the scalars x and y are called the components of \(vec v\). When we need to perform calculations with vectors, we usually describe them as standard-position vectors because it is easier to perform such calculations.

1.1.6 Relationship between vector components and its magnitude and orientation

To find vector magnitude from its components, we use the Pythagorean Theorem. And to find its direction we use the inverse tangent function.

Given a position vector \(vec v = (x, y)\), the magnitude (or module) is found by

\begin{flalign} \Vert\vec v\Vert\ = \sqrt{x^2+y^2} \label{eq:6_1} \end{flalign}

And the direction is found by

\begin{flalign} \theta = \arctan (y/x) \label{eq:6_2} \end{flalign}

Given the magnitude and direction of a vector `vecv`, its x component is found by

\begin{flalign} x = \Vert\vec v\Vert\cos\theta \label{eq:6_3} \end{flalign}

and its y component is found by

\begin{flalign} y = \Vert\vec v\Vert\sin\theta \label{eq:6_4} \end{flalign}

1.1.7 Vector addition and scalar multiplication using vector components

The sum of two vectors given in its component form, \(\vec v = (x_1, y_1)\) and \(\vec w = (x_2, y_2)\), is defined as

\begin{flalign} \vec v + \vec w = (x_1 + x_2, y_1 + y_2) \label{eq:7_1} \end{flalign}

The product of an scalar \( \lambda \) and a vector \( \vec v \), given in its component form, \( \vec v = (x, y) \), is defined as

\begin{flalign} \lambda\vec v = (\lambda x, \lambda y) \label{eq:7_2} \end{flalign}

1.1.8 The unit vector

The unit vector \(\vec u_v\) of a non cero vector \(\vec v\) is a vector that has the same direction as \(\vec v\) but a magnitude of 1. the unit vector is calculated as.

\begin{flalign} \vec u_v = \vec v/\Vert\vec v\Vert \label{eq:8_1} \end{flalign}

Note that vector magnitude \(\Vert\vec v\Vert\) is an scalar, and dividing by a scalar is the same as multiplying by the reciprocal of the scalar \(1/\Vert\vec v\Vert \).

1.1.9 Vector representation with unitary vectors \(\hat{\imath}\), \(\hat{\jmath}\) and \(\hat{k}\)

In a two-dimensional space, a vector can be represented using unit vectors \(\hat{\imath}\) and \(\hat{\jmath}\). The unit vector \(\hat{\imath}\) is a vector of length 1 along the x-axis, and the unit vector \(\hat{\jmath}\) is a vector of length 1 along the y-axis.

If we have a vector given in its component form \(\vec v = (x, y)\), it can be represented as:

\begin{flalign} \vec v = x\hat{\imath} + y\hat{\jmath} \label{eq:9_1} \end{flalign}

In a three-dimensional space, a vector can be represented using unit vectors \(\hat{\imath}\), \(\hat{\jmath}\) and \(\vec k\). The unit vector \(\hat{\imath}\) is a vector of length 1 along the x-axis, the unit vector \(\hat{\jmath}\) is a vector of length 1 along the y-axis, and the unit vector \(\hat{k}\) is a vector of length 1 along the z-axis.

If we have a vector given in its component form \(\vec v = (x, y, z)\), it can be represented as:

\begin{flalign} \vec v = x\hat{\imath} + y\hat{\jmath} + z\hat{k} \label{eq:9_2} \end{flalign}

This vector sum is called a linear combination of the vectors \(\hat{\imath}\), \(\hat{\jmath}\) and \(\hat{k}\).

1.1.10 Scalar product of two vectors

The scalar product (also known as the dot product) of two vectors is a number (scalar) that is obtained by multiplying the corresponding components of the two vectors and then adding those products.

If \(\vec v = (x_1, y_1, z_1)\) and \(\vec w = (x_2, y_2, z_2)\) are two vectors, then their scalar product is calculated as follows:

\begin{flalign} \vec v\cdot\vec w = x_1\cdot x_2 + y_1\cdot y_2 + z_1\cdot z_2 \label{eq:10_1} \end{flalign}

In terms of magnitude and direction, the scalar product can also be defined as:

\begin{flalign} \vec v\cdot\vec w = \Vert\vec v\Vert\cdot\Vert\vec w\Vert\cos\theta \label{eq:10_2} \end{flalign}

Where \(\theta\) is the angle between \(\vec v\) and \(\vec w\).

From \eqref{eq:10_2} we can express \(\theta\) as:

\begin{flalign} \theta = \arccos\frac{\vec v\cdot\vec w}{\Vert\vec v\Vert\cdot\Vert\vec w\Vert} \label{eq:10_3} \end{flalign}

When the dot product of two vectors is zero, is said that both vectors are orthogonal or perpendicular.

1.1.11 Vector projection.

The projection of \(\vec w\) onto \(\vec v\) or onto the line that contains \(\vec v\), denoted as \(proj_v \vec w\), is a vector that lies on \(\vec v\) and has a direction same as \(\vec v\) (if the angle between \(\vec v\) and \(\vec w\) is less than 90 degrees) or opposite to \(\vec v\) (if the angle between \(\vec v\) and \(\vec w\) is more than 90 degrees).

Since the projection of \(\vec w\) onto \(\vec v\) is aligned with \(\vec v\):

\begin{flalign} proj_v \vec w = \Vert proj_v \vec w\Vert\cdot\vec u_v \label{eq:11_1} \end{flalign}

From the definition of cosine it follows that the magnitude of this projection vector can be calculated as:

\begin{flalign} \Vert proj_v \vec w\Vert = \Vert\vec w\Vert\cos\theta \label{eq:11_2} \end{flalign}

Where \(\theta\) is the angle between \(\vec v\) and \(\vec w\).

Now, if we consider the expression [1.12] for the dot product of \(\vec v\) and \(\vec w\), the magnitude of \(proj_v \vec w\) can also be expressed as:

\begin{flalign} \Vert proj_v \vec w\Vert = \frac{\vec w\cdot\vec v}{\Vert\vec v\Vert} = \vec w\cdot\vec u_v \label{eq:11_3} \end{flalign}

So, using the coordinates of \(\vec v\), \( (x_1, y_1, z_1)\) and of \(\vec w\), \( (x_2, y_2, z_2) \) to calculate their dot product:

\begin{flalign} \Vert proj_v \vec w\Vert = \frac{x_1\cdot x_2 + y_1\cdot y_2 + z_1\cdot z_2}{\sqrt{x_1^2+y_1^2+z_1^2}} \label{eq:11_4} \end{flalign}

1.1.12 Vector cross product.

The cross product, or vector product, is a binary operation on two vectors in three-dimensional space. It is written as \(\vec v \times \vec w\). The result of the cross product is a vector that is perpendicular (or orthogonal) to the vectors being multiplied or said in other way, normal to the plane containing them.

The direction of the cross product is determined by the right-hand rule. If you point your index finger in the direction of \(\vec v\), and your middle finger in the direction of \(\vec w\), your thumb points in the direction of (\vec v \times \vec w\).

If \(\vec v\) and \(\vec w\) are two vectors, and θ is the angle between them, the magnitude of their cross product is given by:

\begin{flalign} \Vert\vec v \times \vec w\Vert = \Vert\vec v\Vert\cdot\Vert\vec w\Vert\sin\theta \label{eq:12_1} \end{flalign}

Then, the magnitude (or length) of the cross product can be interpreted as the area of the parallelogram that the vectors span.

In terms of its components, if \(\vec v = (x_1, y_1, z_1)\) and \(\vec w = (x_2, y_2, z_2)\), the cross product \(\vec v \times \vec w\) is a vector defined as:

\begin{flalign} \vec v \times \vec w = (y_1 \cdot z_2 - z_1 \cdot y_2, z_1 \cdot x_2 - x_1 \cdot z_2, x_1 \cdot y_2 - y_1 \cdot x_2) \label{eq:12_2} \end{flalign}

Each component of this resulting vector is the determinant of a 2x2 matrix formed by eliminating the row and column of the corresponding component from a 3x3 matrix containing the unit vectors \(\hat{\imath}\), \(\hat{\jmath}\), \(\vec k\) in the first row, the components of \(\vec v\) in the second row, and the components of \(\vec w\) in the third row.

\begin{flalign} \begin{vmatrix}\vec i & \vec j & \vec k \\ x_1 & y_1 & z_1 \\ x_2 & y_2 & z_2\\ \end{vmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix} y_1 & z_1 \\ y_2 & z_2 \\ \end{vmatrix} \hat{\imath} - \begin{vmatrix} x_1 & z_1 \\ x_2 & z_2\\ \end{vmatrix} \hat{\jmath} + \begin{vmatrix} x_1 & y_1 \\ x_2 & y_2 \\ \end{vmatrix}\hat{k} \label{eq:12_3} \end{flalign}

Properties of the cross product:

  • Anticommutative property:
      \(\vec u \times \vec v = -\vec v \times \vec u\)
  • Distributive property:
      \(\vec u \times (\vec v + \vec w) = \vec u \times \vec v + \vec u \times \vec w\)
  • Multiplication by a constant:
      \(c\cdot(\vec u \times \vec v) = (c\cdot\vec u) \times \vec v = \vec u \times (c\cdot\vec v)\)
  • Cross product of the zero vector:
      \(\vec u \times \vec 0 = \vec 0 \times \vec u = \vec 0\)
  • Cross product of a vector with itself:
      \(\vec v \times \vec v = \vec 0\)

1.1.13 Moment of a Sliding Vector about a Point

The moment of a sliding vector \(\vec v\) about a point P is calculated using the cross product of \(\vec r\) by \(\vec v\). Being \(\vec r\) the position vector of \(\vec v\) relative to P

\begin{flalign} \vec M = \vec r \times \vec v \label{eq:13_1} \end{flalign}

The position vector, \(\vec r\), extends from the point of interest P to the origin of \(\vec v\).

The properties of the cross product allow us to conclude that \(\vec M\) is a vector perpendicular to the plane defined by \(\vec r\) and \(\vec v\) and it doesn't change if we move the sliding vector \(\vec v\) along its line of action. This is because the moment of a sliding vector is a measure of its tendency to cause rotation about a point or axis, which is determined by both the vector’s magnitude and its distance from the point or axis.

When you slide a vector along its line of action, you’re not changing the sliding vector’s magnitude or the perpendicular distance from its line of action to the point or axis of interest. Therefore, the moment remains the same. This property is what makes it a “sliding vector”.

This concept is fundamental in physics and engineering, as it helps us understand and calculate the effects of forces on the rotation of objects.

1.1.14 Moment of a Sliding Vector About a Line

The moment of a sliding vector about a line of interest l is the projection onto said line of the moment of the vector with respect to any point on the line.

The magnitude of the moment of a sliding vector about a line l, \(\Vert \vec M_l\Vert \) can be calculated using the cross and dot product of three vectors:

  1. Unit vector along the line of interest l, \(\vec u_l = (u_{lx}, u_{ly}, u_{lz})\).
  2. Position vector \(\vec r = (r_x, r_y, r_z)\) from any point along the line of interest l to any point on the line of action of the sliding vector \(\vec v\).
  3. The sliding vector \(\vec v = (v_x, v_y, v_z)\).
That is:

\begin{flalign} \Vert\vec M_l\Vert = \vec u_l \cdot (\vec r \times \vec v) \label{eq:14_1} \end{flalign}

To streamline the calculations even further, mathematically we can combine the cross and dot product into what is known as a mixed triple product. In the mixed triple product we write the cross product as a determinant in the form:

\begin{flalign} \Vert\vec M_l\Vert = \vec u_l \cdot (\vec r \times \vec v) = \begin{vmatrix}u_{lx} & u_{ly} & u_{lz} \\ r_x & r_y & r_z \\ v_x & v_y & v_z\\ \end{vmatrix} = u_{lx}\cdot\begin{vmatrix} r_y & r_z \\ v_y & v_z \\ \end{vmatrix} - u_{ly}\cdot\begin{vmatrix} r_x & r_z \\ v_x & v_z \\ \end{vmatrix} + u_{lz}\cdot\begin{vmatrix} r_x & r_y \\ v_x & v_y \\ \end{vmatrix} \label{eq:14_2} \end{flalign}

Finally, since a vector can be expressed as the product of its modulus and its unit vector, we have:

\begin{flalign} \vec M_l = \Vert\vec M_l\Vert \cdot \vec u_l \label{eq:14_3} \end{flalign}

1.1.15 Vector Functions

A vector-valued function, also referred to as a vector function, is a mathematical function of one or more variables whose range is a set of vectors.

For example, a common type of vector-valued function is one that depends on a single real parameter t and produces a vector \(\vec f(t)\) as the result. In terms of the standard unit vectors \(\hat{\imath}\), \(\hat{\jmath}\) and \(\hat{k}\) of Cartesian 3D-space, these specific types of vector-valued functions are given by expressions such as:

\begin{flalign} \vec f(t) = f_x(t)\hat{\imath} + f_y(t)\hat{\jmath} + f_z(t)\hat{k} \label{eq:15_1} \end{flalign}

Where \(f_x(t)\), \(f_y(t)\), and \(f_z(t)\) are the coordinate functions of the parameter t, and the domain of this vector-valued function is the intersection of the domains of the functions \(f_x(t)\), \(f_y(t)\), and \(f_z(t)\).

1.1.16 Derivative of a vector-valued function

The derivative of a vector-valued function is nearly identical to the definition of derivative of a real-valued function of one variable. However, because the range of a vector-valued function consists of vectors, the same is true for the range of the derivative of a vector-valued function. That is:

\begin{flalign} \vec f'(t) = \frac{d\vec f}{dt} = \frac{df_x}{dt}\hat{\imath} + \frac{df_y}{dt}\hat{\jmath} +\frac{df_z}{dt}\hat{k} \label{eq:16_1} \end{flalign}

1.1.17 Partial derivative of a function and its Total derivative.

If a function depends of more than one variable, its partial derivative is the derivative with respect to one of those variables, keeping the others as constants.

The partial derivative of a function \(f(x,y,z)\) respect of one variable, x, is represented as:

\begin{flalign} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \label{eq:17_1} \end{flalign}

The total derivative of the function \(f(x,y,z)\) can be calculated using the expression:

\begin{flalign} df = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}dx + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}dy + \frac{\partial f}{\partial z}dz \label{eq:17_2} \end{flalign}

1.1.18 Integral of a vector-valued function

To find the integral of a vector-valued function, we usually split it into its components and integrate each component separately. That is, if \(\vec f(t) = f_x(t)\vec i + f_y(t)\vec j + f_z(t)\vec k = (f_x(t), f_y(t), f_z(t)) \), its integral is:

\begin{flalign} \int \vec f(t) dt = \int (f_x(t), f_y(t), f_z(t)) dt = \left( \int f_x(t)dt, \int f_y(t)dt, \int f_z(t)dt \right) \label{eq:18_1} \end{flalign}

Exercise 1

A boatman is rowing on the boat, wanting to always stay perpendicular to the river bank and crossing with an average speed of km/h. The river water flows with a speed of km/h. How fast should the boat be propelled? In what direction?

The velocity of the boat \(\vec v_b\) is the sum of the velocity with which the boat \(\vec v_p\) is propelled by the boatman plus the velocity of the river water \(\vec v_r\). Then:

  \(\vec v_p = \vec v_b - \vec v_r \),

If we choose the coordinate plane so that the x-axis has the same direction as the river, the vector components are:

  \(\vec v_b\) = () Km/h,

  \(\vec v_r\) = () Km/h,

  \(\vec v_p\) = () - () = () Km/h,

The magnitude of the vector \(\vec v_p\) is the speed at which the boat is propelled, is expressed as \(\Vert\vec v_p\Vert\) and, according to \eqref{eq:6_1}, is calculated as:

  \(\Vert\vec v_p\Vert = \sqrt{v_x^2 + v_y^2} = \sqrt{9^2 + 36^2} = \sqrt{81+1296} = \sqrt {1377}\) = Km/h.

The value of the angle φ is the direction in which the boat is propelled, corresponds to the orientation of \(\vec v_p\) and according to \eqref{eq:10_3}, is calculated as:

  $$\phi = \arccos\frac{\Vert\vec v_p\Vert\cdot\Vert\vec v_r\Vert}{\vec v_p\cdot\vec v_r}$$

  \(\phi = \)º

So, the answer to the exercise is that the boat must be propeled at a speed of Km/h and in a direction that forms º with the river.
